Revive food ordering

Overview

Beta Acid, a Brooklyn based agency, was contracted by Aurify to help pull three up-and-coming fast-casual brands– Melt Shop, Little Beet, and Fields Good Chicken– out of a Covid slump by building out fully online ordering experiences.

Pre-pandemic, these startups thrived off of sales from their brick and mortar stores. Revenue suffered when they had to close down stores and rely almost exclusively on third-party delivery platforms (e.g. Grubhub, Uber Eats, Postmates).

Beta Acid needed a designer to drive the creation of customer-facing mobile applications that would fully integrate with each brand’s existing tech stack, including menu management, loyalty programs, and point-of-sale systems.

Role & Duration

  • Lead Designer: Research, wireframing, prototyping; project & stakeholder management.
  • Team: Tech lead, Design lead, supporting engineers & design
  • 12 weeks: July - Sep 2020

Challenge

Design native food ordering applications in 3ish months for Melt Shop, Little Beet, and Fields Good Chicken. Tailor the UX to fit the unique needs of each brand, its menu offerings, and its customers.

Understanding Drop off

As you can imagine, most first-time customers who land on the Payment page bounce (especially on mobile web). This could be for various reasons, like wanting to shop around for a better deal or not having a credit card on hand.

Identifying drop off points and contextualizing the data with qualitative user research (e.g. customer calls) helped us identify opportunities to engage customers more effectively through each phase of the purchase funnel.

Usability Testing

Since I was working off existing designs, I began my process by evaluating the current checkout flow to identify baselines for improvement. Specifically, I wanted to learn: are there usability issues that may cause confusion or hesitation? Do users’ expectations for a cleaning service align with the information presented to them? Are there lingering questions left unanswered?

Usability testing helped me better understand how users engage with the current checkout flow and identity some points of friction.

Comp Analysis

Throughout the initiative I took a bunch of competitor screenshots to help visualize the landscape. I looked at direct competitors as well as companies that sell subscriptions, like fitness classes and meal plans. One common pattern among competitors was multi-page checkout flows.

I found this to be interesting given the increased likelihood that customers would abandon their purchase with every added page. The multi-page trend suggests a clear benefit: customers likely prefer to be taken step-by-step through a checkout experience, allowing them to carefully think through their purchase and confirm all necessary information.


What if customers didn't have to pay?

Simple UI changes can only move the needle so much. What if we could create a shopping experience where customers could reserve a cleaning appointment without paying?



Learnings & Reflection


Learnings from tests

No single test can prove or disprove a hypothesis. Most of the tests were determined as inconclusive. This didn’t mean that our hypothesis was wrong but that we need more data / more testing to understand.

Take customer feedback with a grain of salt. What people say and do are not always aligned and it’s helpful to test strategies and assumptions even when you think they’re not likely to succeed.

Think like a psychologist. Understanding the customer mindset is absolutely necessary to improve business growth, and mindsets can change from day-to-day.

closing thoughts

Designing for growth comes down to core principles of psychology and human behavior. We were challenged to put ourselves in the shoes of the customer and come up with creative ways to influence purchase behavior. However, in order to drive meaningful change, we would need to gain customer trust in our service and brand- which extends well beyond the checkout flow.

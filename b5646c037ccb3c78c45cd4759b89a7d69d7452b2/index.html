Streamlining Physician Oversight

Overview

TrialSpark is a biotech company whose mission is to bring treatment to patients faster by solving some of the biggest operational challenges of clinical trials, from recruiting subjects to standardizing the collection of data.

My team, known as Trial Operations (TrialOps), worked on the company’s proprietary software, called Pilot, used primarily by research coordinators to capture clinical subject data. This data eventually gets sent to the FDA for final review.

As one of two designers on the TrialOps team, I focused on addressing the thousands of hours lost each year by research coordinators waiting to collect in-person signatures from physicians.

Role & Duration

  • Designer: Research, wireframing, testing & prototyping
  • Squad: 5 engineers, 2 designers, 1 PM
  • 12 weeks: Feb - May 2020

Industry Definitions

Clinical Research Coordinator (CRC): someone with a research/medical background who facilitates and coordinates daily clinical trial activities. CRCs are responsible for conducting patient visits in accordance with the study protocol and managing the collection of patient data throughout a study.

Primary Investigator (PI): a physician/MD who supervises the conduct of a study at a given site. PIs are responsible for overseeing patient visits, ensuring the safety and well-being of the study participant. They provide clinical judgment when necessary and sign off on paper and electronic forms where subject data is captured.

Some Context

TrialSpark brings clinical trials to the local doctor’s office. Traditionally, clinical research is conducted in large academic hospitals and universities. This means that patients who don’t live in cities near these institutions often never learn about or have access to clinical trials that directly target their disease. TrialSpark diversifies access by assembling a team of roaming researchers who travel directly to doctors’ local practices to set up trial sites.

This new model has its challenges:

1. Aligning CRC and PI schedules. At TrialSpark, research coordinators are not permanently stationed at one site (often travelling to multiple in a week), which makes aligning schedules with PIs especially difficult.

2. Working with research naive sites. Doctors who participate in TrialSpark studies do not have dedicated daily research personnel (nurses, resident students, disease specialists etc.) to carry out the enormous workload involved in conducting studies. This puts the unfair burden on CRCs to carry out administrative and communication responsibilities normally delegated to PIs and other research staff.

3. Getting PIs to use TrialSpark technology independently. TrialSpark PIs are not accustomed to using clinical research software, further relying on the help of research coordinators.


For a given study...

  • TrialSpark runs ~10-15 sites.
  • TrialSpark CRCs are assigned to 2-3 sites.
  • Each site has a designated PI and ~3-5 enrolled subjects.
  • Each subject has ~20 visits.
  • Each visit has ~20 forms that require signatures.
  • PIs are responsible for ~400 forms per subject, ~1400 forms per site.
  • CRCs wait ~2.5 hours after a visit to collect in-person PI signatures.
  • ~3500 hours wasted.

Some more context

When I started at TrialSpark, physicians were not accustomed to using Pilot software independently to sign off on forms. Instead, they relied on research coordinators to help them sign in and navigate to the appropriate forms that required action. Pilot, at the time, was simply not built to support physicians’ workflows. For example, there were no visual indicators from the homepage directing physicians to the specific forms that required their review. Moreover, the system lacked simple microinteractions, like tooltips and progress indicators, to make it easier for users to understand where to go and anticipate what will happen next.

Obviously this was inefficient. Coordinators had to repeatedly wait long hours (5+) after a patient visit to meet with physicians to get signatures. This was especially inconvenient because TrialSpark coordinators were not permanently stationed at these sites. Though securing signatures at the end of a patient visit was the ideal scenario, this was rarely feasible.

Then COVID-19 happened, which sparked a team-wide initiative to support and streamline remote signoff.


Problem Framing

PIs are unable to electronically sign off on study forms independently. This is problematic because:

1. CRCs are blocked without these signatures, causing them to wait unnecessarily long hours after a patient visit to physically meet with PIs to get signoff.

2. Remote patient visits are becoming more common, which means PIs and CRCs need to be able to execute all study tasks autonomously.


How might we support and streamline remote signoff without compromising patient safety and data integrity?


Current: no signature status indicators exist, so user has to check each subject and subsequent form individually to identify whether a form is signed or needs a signature.


Proposed: signature status indicators on each page help users navigate to the appropriate forms that require signatures.

Research & Synthesis

If my team was to improve the electronic signature flow, we needed to understand the regulations around PI oversight: Why are signatures necessary? To what extent can we change the status quo?

Regulation vs Guidelines

There is a lot of ambiguity around the interpretation of Good Clinical Practice (GCP). When it comes to physician oversight, the FDA establishes general guidelines that can be translated broadly and (too often) inconsistently.

This became apparent to me when interviewing CRCs who had varied experiences working with auditors and data monitors at the site level. I quickly discovered that varying degrees of leniency ultimately affect how coordinators approach physician oversight.

For example, some CRCs held very conservative views on PI oversight (e.g. it’s necessary that my PI looks over and signs off on all forms in a timely manner to ensure accuracy and proper oversight, especially if an auditor were to investigate), while others seemed more lax (e.g. unless there’s an urgent matter, I don’t think it’s necessary for my PI to sign off on every form).

As a company, we constantly have to weigh the potential benefits and risks of efficiency over thoroughness.

User Pain Points

Whenever I spoke with CRCs, I was reminded just how frustrating it was to track down PIs for signatures.

One time I waited like 5 hours just to get one last signature. It was really frustrating. - CRC

I literally have to quadruple check [Pilot] to ensure all forms have been signed off. This can mean clicking into 20 forms sometimes... There’s no way around it. - CRC

I don’t trust that my PI can navigate through Source without accidentally modifying data. I feel better helping him in-person so that I can answer any questions. My PI is smart, obviously, but not the most tech-savvy. - CRC


Solving CRCs' frustrations became my underlying motivation.


Ideate & Review

Next, I began sketching and wireframing, looping in my PM and engineers early on and continuously throughout the process.

Some ideas that we pursued early on were around consolidating signoff. For example, having one signature apply to all visit forms rather than requiring PIs to sign off on multiple forms. We then discussed conditional signatures, such that a PI would only have to sign a form with “high-risk” data. We had several meetings with key stakeholders discussing the appropriate level of physician oversight.

While it would have been ideal to create a visit-level/ ‘sign all’ capability to expedite signoff, we weren’t able to get buy-in from clinical team members who felt strongly that PIs need to review each form individually. Here, the ongoing battle between efficiency and thoroughness swayed towards thoroughness.

Though we weren’t aligned (just yet) on a ‘sign all’ feature, I set out to solve the known pain points with the existing flow:

#1 Call-to-action

Signature button is not readily accessible (user has to scroll to the bottom of the form to sign), and the relationship between ‘save and continue’ and ‘sign’ is confusing (unclear which to select first).

Solution:

  • Position primary actions at top right of page.
  • Clearly distinguish save vs sign (only enable one at a time) so there’s no confusion around which action is needed when.

#2 Credentials

The FDA requires that PIs re-enter their credentials after each signature. Yet, the current system requires the user to click into the input field to begin typing. Additionally, if PIs forget their password, there is no easy way to reset it from the signoff modal.

Solution:

  • Make the password input field active when the page loads, so that as soon as the page has loaded the user can begin typing.
  • Include easy access to password reset flow (case study coming soon!).

#3 Feedback

After signing a form, PI does not receive any visual indicators that the signature was successfully executed and is automatically brought to the next form on the list (which doesn’t necessarily require a signature). PI then has to navigate to the side panel to find the next form that requires action.

Solution:

  • Provide feedback to the user when a form is signed
  • Aggregate forms that require signatures to the top of the side panel, defaulting to the next form that requires action once the previous form has been signed.

Test & Iterate

I used weekly product/design syncs to validate solutions and refine accordingly. Mainly, these meetings helped ensure that what I was proposing was in-scope. I looped in CRCs during the later phase of my design work, often to uncover usability issues and opportunities for improvement.


Status label iterations

I went through a series of iterations to figure out the most effective visual treatment that would easily guide the user to each form that requires action.

The status labels needed to clearly show which forms require signatures and further differentiate those that are ready to be signed (e.g. data is complete) from those that are not ready (e.g. data is partially entered).

Text vs Icon

Feedback: The “needs signature” label requires less cognitive effort to understand than a PI icon

Signed Label vs Empty State

Feedback: signed status is actually more distracting than useful; visual emphasis should be reserved for the action that needs to be done.

Muted “Needs Signature” label vs Empty State

Feedback: A subtle indicator when a form needs signature (even if it’s not ready) is more helpful than having none.

Winner

Text labels; visual emphasis for what can be actioned now; subtle emphasis for what will need to be actioned later.


Refine & Ship

After a series of iterations, here’s what the team landed on:

Email. PI receives an email after a visit, prompting him to log in and sign off on visit forms. The email takes the PI directly to the subject’s Source page.



Sign-in. PI signs in, which takes him to the Subject List page (homepage). The table shows which subjects have pending signatures. PI can hover over a subject row, click on “more” and see which visits have pending signatures.

Learnings & Reflection

After each design sprint, my team liked to debrief with an exercise called “I like, I wish, I wonder…” to gather open and honest feedback. Here are my takeaways:

I like that design and engineering collaborated closely and efficiently so that we could incorporate the new UI system into the feature rollout. I also like that we were able to involve Clinical Research Coordinators (CRCs) in the design process, ensuring that we were addressing their pain points head on.

I wish that we could have challenged the status quo by making more meaningful changes to the signature flow (e.g. batch signoff, sign via mobile instead of desktop, eliminating signatures altogether). However, this would have required much more time and cross-functional effort to align with clinical study team stakeholders and could have risked negatively impacting our relationship with conservative pharmaceutical companies (trial sponsors).

I wonder how long it would have taken us to prioritize these changes if COVID-19 had not impacted our ability to conduct in-person studies. Though CRC and PI user pain points had existed for a long time, there wasn’t a strong enough business reason to divert engineering resources towards addressing them. However, given how quickly the world around us can change the needs of a business and its users, it is even more important to be able to adapt quickly.
